How Does NOUS Help You?

NOUS estimates what's happening in your mind from what comes out of your mouth. Sixty seconds of speech or writing produces a twelve-sector cognitive map, a severity score, and — over time — a trajectory of change. Three pathways serve three purposes: CI for clinical assessment, CSAA for forensic authenticity, VOCA for longitudinal monitoring. One platform. One theory. Three ways to see what was previously invisible.

For the Clinician

CI — Cognitive Intelligence

You spend the first session trying to understand what's happening with your patient. They talk for an hour. You form an impression. NOUS gives you a quantitative cognitive signature from sixty seconds of that conversation — twelve sectors scored, a severity classification, treatment protocol matching, and a baseline you can track over time. It doesn't replace your clinical judgment. It gives you a measurement instrument where before you had only observation.

For the Forensic Professional

CSAA — Cognitive Signature Authenticity Assessment

You receive a statement and need to determine whether it's consistent with genuine experience or shows signs of cognitive construction. NOUS analyzes the linguistic signature across every segment of the narrative, producing an Authenticity Index and a Chain of Evidence you can present in proceedings designed to meet Daubert standards. It doesn't tell you if someone is lying. It tells you whether the cognitive architecture behind their words matches what we'd expect from someone who lived what they're describing.

For the Monitoring Professional

VOCA — Vocal Oscillation Cognitive Archive

You need to know if someone is getting better, getting worse, or staying the same — and you need to know it objectively, not from self-report. NOUS tracks cognitive signatures over time, detects drift from baseline, and predicts integration trajectories. It applies the same mathematics that describes how massive systems absorb new material to show how the human mind processes new experience. You see the trajectory. You see whether intervention is working. You see it before the patient can tell you.
